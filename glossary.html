No. 007 — Reference

AI glossary, in plain English

The terms that get thrown around in every AI product page and news article, defined without circular jargon. Jump to one, or read top to bottom.

LLM Token Prompt Context window Hallucination Fine-tuning RAG Agent Multimodal Temperature System prompt Parameters
LLM (Large Language Model)
The type of AI model behind tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. It's trained on huge amounts of text to predict what word (or piece of a word) is likely to come next, and that simple mechanism, at large enough scale, produces the fluent, useful text these tools generate.
Token
The small chunk of text a model actually processes — often a word, part of a word, or a punctuation mark. Models read and generate text one token at a time, and usage limits/pricing are often measured in tokens rather than words.
Prompt
The input you give a model — a question, instruction, or piece of text you want it to work with. "Prompt engineering" just means writing that input more clearly and specifically to get a better result.
Context window
The amount of text a model can "see" and reference at once, including your current prompt plus recent conversation history or an attached document. Ask something that refers back to a point far outside that window, and the model genuinely won't have access to it anymore.
Hallucination
When a model states something false or made-up with the same fluent confidence as something true — a fabricated statistic, a citation that doesn't exist, or a wrong fact. It's a known limitation of how these models generate text, which is why fact-checking matters most on anything you didn't already know the answer to.
Fine-tuning
Taking an already-trained model and further training it on a narrower, specific dataset so it performs better on a particular task or in a particular style — for example, a customer-support tone or a specific technical domain.
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
A technique where a model looks up relevant information from an outside source — a document, database, or the web — before generating its answer, rather than relying only on what it learned during training. This is part of how tools like Perplexity ground answers in current, citable sources.
Agent / agentic
A step beyond a simple chatbot: an AI system that can take multiple actions on its own toward a goal — running code, browsing, calling other tools — rather than just replying to one prompt at a time. Coding tools like Replit Agent fall into this category.
Multimodal
A model that can work with more than just text — understanding or generating images, audio, or video in addition to written language, rather than being limited to a single format.
Temperature
A setting (mostly used by developers via an API, less often in consumer apps) that controls how predictable versus varied a model's output is. Lower temperature gives more consistent, conservative answers; higher temperature gives more varied, sometimes more creative — but also less predictable — output.
System prompt
Background instructions given to a model before your conversation starts, usually invisible to you, that shape its behavior, tone, or rules — for example, telling it what role to play or what it shouldn't do.
Parameters
The internal numerical values a model learns during training that determine how it processes input and produces output. "Parameter count" is often used as a rough (and imperfect) proxy for a model's scale — more parameters doesn't automatically mean better in every task.
