How to choose an AI writing tool (without wasting a month trialing all of them)

Published Jul 1, 2026 · 6 min read

Most people pick an AI writing tool by whichever one a coworker mentioned last week. That works fine until the tool turns out to be built for a different job than the one you actually have — a grammar checker won't write your ad copy, and a copywriting tool will over-polish an email that just needed a typo fixed.

Here's a faster way to narrow it down: instead of asking "which tool is best," ask "which of these three jobs am I actually doing this week?"

Job 1: You're editing something you already wrote

If the words mostly exist and you need them cleaner — fewer run-ons, a more consistent tone, fewer typos — you want an editing layer, not a generator. Tools like Grammarly sit on top of what you type rather than replacing it, which matters if the voice already sounds like you and you just want it sharper. The test: if you'd be uncomfortable with the tool rewriting a full paragraph on its own, you're in this category.

Job 2: You're producing marketing copy on a schedule

Ad variations, landing page headlines, product descriptions at volume — this is repeatable, template-shaped work, and it's exactly what tools like Jasper and Copy.ai are built around. The trade-off is that generated copy in this category tends to sound generic until you feed it real examples of your brand voice first. Budget time for that setup, or the output will read like everyone else's ads.

Job 3: You're starting from a blank page

Long-form drafting, working through an argument, or thinking out loud through a first draft is a different job again, and it's where general-purpose assistants like ChatGPT or Claude tend to outperform the narrower tools — because the task isn't "produce copy," it's "think alongside me," and that needs a model that can hold context and push back, not just fill in a template.

A quick way to decide

  • If you'd only trust it to tidy up your own words — pick an editing tool.
  • If you need the same style of output repeated across dozens of variations — pick a copywriting tool, and feed it brand examples first.
  • If you don't know exactly what you want to say yet — pick a general-purpose assistant and think through it in conversation.

None of this is permanent. Plenty of people use two of these at once — draft in a general assistant, then run the result through an editing tool before it goes out. The mistake isn't using multiple tools, it's picking one because it's popular and expecting it to do a job it wasn't built for.
